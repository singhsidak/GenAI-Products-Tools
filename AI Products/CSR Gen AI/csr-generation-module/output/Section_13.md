# 13. Discussion and Overall Conclusions

The pivotal clinical study demonstrated that the BNT162b2 vaccine is highly efficacious in preventing COVID-19, with an acceptable safety profile across the studied populations. This section provides a summary and discussion of these findings, their clinical relevance, identified uncertainties, and implications for future considerations.

# 13.1 Efficacy Results

The study demonstrated substantial efficacy in preventing symptomatic COVID-19 infection. For subjects aged 12 to 15 years, vaccine efficacy against the first COVID-19 occurrence from 7 days after Dose 2 was 100.0% (95% CI: 86.8%, 100.0%). This was based on 0 cases in the BNT162b2 group (n=1057) compared to 28 cases in the placebo group (n=1030) within the surveillance time.

For the broader population including participants 16 years of age and older, vaccine efficacy for the first primary endpoint against confirmed COVID-19 occurring at least 7 days after Dose 2 was 95.0% (95% credible interval: 90.3%, 97.6%). Efficacy was also observed after the first dose, with an estimated VE of 82% (95% CI: 75.6%, 86.9%) against confirmed COVID-19 occurring after Dose 1 of the two-dose regimen. The onset of protection was observed approximately 14 days after Dose 1, as evidenced by the divergence of cumulative incidence curves between vaccine and placebo groups.

Vaccine efficacy remained high, exceeding 93%, across various subgroups defined by age, sex, race/ethnicity, and country for the first primary efficacy endpoint. This was consistent, with the exception of the "all others" race group (89.3% VE) and Brazil (87.7% VE). In participants with comorbidities, VE was consistently greater than 91% across all analyzed risk subgroups.

Reactogenicity and adverse events (AEs) were typically milder and less frequent in older participants (≥56 years of age) compared to younger participants (≤55 years of age) and younger adolescents (12 to 15 years of age). Reactogenicity was mostly mild to moderate and short-lived.

# 13.3 New or Unexpected Findings and Inconsistencies

Several notable findings emerged from the safety data. Lymphadenopathy was reported more frequently in the vaccine group (0.4%) compared to placebo (0.0%), and nausea was also more common with the vaccine (0.8% vs 0.2%). These events are considered biologically plausible given the mechanism of vaccination.

A more significant finding was the reporting of acute peripheral paralysis in 4 cases in the vaccine group versus 0 in the placebo group across the whole study population. Of these, 2 cases were considered related to the study treatment, indicating a reasonable possibility of a causal relationship. This requires careful consideration and will be included as an Adverse Event of Special Interest (AESI) for pharmacovigilance monitoring.

While not observed within the controlled clinical study, three post-marketing cases of anaphylaxis were reported during vaccination campaigns, all of which resolved with standard treatment. This highlights the importance of administering the vaccine in settings with resuscitation facilities and contraindicating a second dose in individuals who experience anaphylaxis to the first.

Uncertainties also exist regarding the pharmaceutical characterization of the commercial product, specifically related to the presence of truncated/modified forms of mRNA and lipid-related impurities. Although these impurities were present in vaccine batches used in Phase 3 trials and are not currently considered a safety issue based on toxicological principles, further characterization and data are required post-authorization to confirm the adequacy of specifications.

# 13.4 Clinical Relevance and Importance of Results

The demonstrated high efficacy of the BNT162b2 vaccine against symptomatic COVID-19 is highly clinically relevant, offering a critical tool to combat the ongoing global pandemic. The consistent efficacy across diverse subgroups underscores its broad applicability.

Despite the strong efficacy against symptomatic disease, the study had limitations in evaluating efficacy against severe COVID-19 due to a limited number of events, yielding an imprecise estimate (66.4% VE; 95% CI: -124.8%, 96.3%). Efficacy against asymptomatic infection and prevention of SARS-CoV-2 transmission were not primary endpoints and require further investigation post-authorization through epidemiological or specific clinical studies.

The observed early onset of protection after Dose 1 suggests a rapid immune response, which is important for public health strategies. However, the current follow-up duration of approximately 100 days after Dose 1 limits conclusions on long-term protection, with 2-year follow-up data expected to address this.

# 13.5 Benefits, Precautions, and Implications for Future Studies

# 13.5.1 Specific Benefits and Precautions for Individual Subjects or At-Risk Groups

The benefit-risk balance is overwhelmingly positive for subjects at risk of severe COVID-19, including the elderly and those with comorbid conditions. Efficacy is also anticipated in individuals seropositive for SARS-CoV-2 at baseline, although direct data in this group remain limited.

Specific precautions are necessary for certain populations:
- **Pregnant Women:** There are no efficacy or safety data in pregnant women. While a protective effect is anticipated and pregnancy is a risk factor for severe COVID-19, vaccination should be considered on a case-by-case basis. Pregnancy outcomes in study participants will continue to be followed.
- **Immunocompromised Individuals:** Data in immunocompromised subjects are limited (e.g., 196 participants with stable HIV infection). Efficacy may not be as robust as in immunocompetent individuals. However, no particular safety issues were detected, and the benefit/risk balance is deemed positive due to the underlying increased risk of severe COVID-19 in this population.
- **Allergic Reactions:** Hypersensitivity to the active substance or any excipients is a contraindication. The vaccine must be administered in settings where resuscitation facilities are immediately available. A second dose is contraindicated for individuals who experience anaphylaxis to the first dose.
- **Acute Peripheral Paralysis:** Due to the observed imbalance, acute peripheral paralysis will be closely monitored as an AESI in pharmacovigilance and active surveillance studies.

# 13.5.2 Implications for the Conduct of Future Studies

Several areas require further investigation to enhance the understanding of BNT162b2:
- **Long-Term Efficacy and Safety:** The ongoing pivotal Phase 3 study (C4591001) will continue to collect 2-year long-term data to confirm sustained efficacy and safety.
- **Specific Safety Concerns:** Studies are planned to monitor potential safety concerns such as autoimmune disorders and vaccine-associated enhanced disease (VAED). Facial paralysis will be an AESI for monitoring.
- **Efficacy Against Transmission and Asymptomatic Infection:** Post-authorization epidemiological or specific clinical studies are needed to assess the vaccine's effect on SARS-CoV-2 shedding and transmission, particularly from asymptomatic infections.
- **Pharmaceutical Characterization:** Additional data are required post-approval to complete the characterization of the active substance and finished product, addressing uncertainties regarding mRNA integrity, truncated forms, and lipid impurities.
- **Interactions with Other Vaccines:** Interaction studies with other vaccines have not been performed and will be conducted post-authorization.
- **Special Populations:** Further data are needed for immunocompromised individuals, pregnant women, and very elderly subjects through long-term pharmacoepidemiology safety studies.

# 13.6 Impact of Exclusions on Generalisability

The study population, while diverse across age, sex, race/ethnicity, and country subgroups, had certain limitations that affect generalisability. The study was conducted entirely within the USA.

The efficacy data for individuals aged 16-17 years was largely extrapolated from young adults due to the small number of cases in this specific age group within the trial. This limits direct evidence of efficacy for this narrow age bracket. Furthermore, the number of participants with stable HIV infection (196 subjects) and seropositive for SARS-CoV-2 at baseline was limited, preventing comprehensive assessment of efficacy and safety in these groups. Similarly, data for pregnant women were very limited at the time of the analysis.
