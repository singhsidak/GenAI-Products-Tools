# 9. Investigational Plan

# 9.1 Overall Study Plan and Design

This was a Phase 3, multicentre, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group study to evaluate the efficacy and safety of a single, flexible dose of galantamine prolonged-release (PR) capsule (16 or 24 mg/day) in patients with mild to moderate Alzheimer’s Disease (AD).

The study consisted of three periods:
1.  **Screening Period:** A 2-week period to determine patient eligibility.
2.  **Double-blind Treatment Period:** A 6-month (26-week) period during which patients were randomised to receive either galantamine PR or placebo.
3.  **Follow-up Period:** A 4-week follow-up period after the last dose of study medication.

Patients were randomised in a 1:1 ratio to one of two treatment arms:
-   **Group 1:** Galantamine PR (8 mg/day for 4 weeks, then 16 mg/day. At Week 8, the dose could be increased to 24 mg/day based on the investigator's judgment of tolerability and efficacy).
-   **Group 2:** Placebo.

The study employed a double-blind design, where patients, investigators, and sponsor personnel involved in the study conduct were blinded to the treatment assignment. A placebo control was used to establish the efficacy of galantamine PR in the specified patient population. Randomisation was stratified by Apolipoprotein E4 (ApoE4) carrier status (carrier vs. non-carrier).

# 9.2 Rationale for Study Design

The study was designed as a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, which is the gold standard for demonstrating the efficacy of a new treatment. This design was chosen to minimise bias and to allow for a clear comparison of the effects of galantamine PR against placebo.

**Control Group:** A placebo control was used as there was a need to demonstrate the absolute efficacy of galantamine PR. The use of a placebo is considered ethically acceptable in this patient population for a study of this duration, especially given the availability of rescue medication if a patient's condition worsened significantly.

**Blinding:** A double-blind design was implemented to prevent bias in treatment administration, patient response, and the evaluation of outcomes by both the patient and the investigator.

**Dose Selection:** The study used a flexible-dose regimen (16 or 24 mg/day) following an initial titration period starting at 8 mg/day. This mimics clinical practice, allowing for dose adjustment based on individual patient tolerability and response, which is crucial for acetylcholinesterase inhibitors known to cause cholinergic side effects. The dose range of 16-24 mg/day was chosen based on results from previous studies with the immediate-release formulation of galantamine, which demonstrated efficacy and acceptable tolerability in this range.

**Patient Population:** The study population was limited to patients with mild to moderate AD, as defined by specific scores on the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) and Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scale. This is the population for which galantamine is intended and where efficacy has been previously demonstrated. Stratification by ApoE4 status was included to explore its potential as a predictor of treatment response, as suggested by prior research.

**Washout Period:** A washout period for any prior AD medications (e.g., other cholinesterase inhibitors or memantine) was required before screening to avoid carry-over effects that could confound the assessment of the investigational product's efficacy.

# 9.3 Selection of Study Population

# 9.3.1 Inclusion Criteria

Patients were required to meet all of the following criteria to be eligible for inclusion in the study:
1.  Male or female, aged 50 to 90 years, inclusive.
2.  A clinical diagnosis of probable Alzheimer’s Disease according to the National Institute of Neurological and Communicative Disorders and Stroke and the Alzheimer’s Disease and Related Disorders Association (NINCDS-ADRDA) criteria.
3.  A score of 10 to 24 (inclusive) on the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) at Screening.
4.  A global score of 1 (mild) or 2 (moderate) on the Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scale at Screening.
5.  Evidence of a gradual onset and progressive decline of memory and cognitive function for at least 6 months prior to Screening.
6.  A Hachinski Ischemic Score of ≤ 4.
7.  Computed Tomography (CT) or Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) scan of the brain performed within 12 months prior to screening consistent with a diagnosis of probable AD.
8.  A reliable caregiver who was willing and able to ensure compliance with the study protocol.
9.  Written informed consent from the patient (if capable) and the patient's legally acceptable representative/caregiver.

# 9.3.2 Exclusion Criteria

Patients were excluded from the study if they met any of the following criteria:
1.  Any major neurological or psychiatric disorder other than Alzheimer's Disease that could contribute to dementia, including but not limited to Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, vascular dementia, major depression, or schizophrenia.
2.  History of stroke or transient ischemic attack within the last 3 months.
3.  Clinically significant, unstable systemic illness (e.g., uncontrolled hypertension, unstable angina, uncontrolled diabetes).
4.  Myocardial infarction within the last 6 months.
5.  Clinically significant abnormalities in laboratory tests, vital signs, or electrocardiogram (ECG) at Screening, as judged by the investigator.
6.  History of alcohol or drug abuse within the past 2 years.
7.  Known hypersensitivity to galantamine or any of its excipients.
8.  Treatment with any other acetylcholinesterase inhibitor or memantine within 30 days of screening.
9.  Use of any investigational drug within 30 days or 5 half-lives (whichever is longer) prior to screening.
10. Females who were pregnant, breastfeeding, or of childbearing potential and not using a medically accepted method of contraception.

The exclusion criteria were designed primarily for patient safety, to exclude individuals with conditions that could be worsened by a cholinergic agent, and to ensure that the study population was appropriate for assessing the efficacy of galantamine PR in AD.

# 9.3.3 Removal of Patients from Therapy or Assessment

Patients could be withdrawn from the study at any time at their own or their caregiver's request, or at the discretion of the investigator for clinical reasons, including:
-   An adverse event that compromised patient safety.
-   Intercurrent illness.
-   Insufficient therapeutic effect as judged by the investigator.
-   Protocol violation or non-compliance.
-   Patient/caregiver decision to withdraw consent.

Patients who discontinued the study treatment were, whenever possible, to complete an early termination visit that included all assessments scheduled for the final visit (Week 26).

# 9.4 Treatments

# 9.4.1 Treatments Administered

Patients were randomised to receive one of the following treatments, administered orally once daily in the morning with food:

-   **Galantamine PR Arm:**
    -   Weeks 1-4: Galantamine PR 8 mg/day
    -   Weeks 5-8: Galantamine PR 16 mg/day
    -   Weeks 9-26: Galantamine PR 16 mg/day or 24 mg/day. The dose could be increased to 24 mg/day at the Week 8 visit at the investigator's discretion, based on an assessment of efficacy and tolerability. The dose could not be decreased back to 16 mg/day after this point.

-   **Placebo Arm:**
    -   Weeks 1-26: Placebo capsules, identical in appearance to the galantamine PR capsules.

# 9.4.2 Identity of Investigational Product(s)

The investigational product was galantamine prolonged-release (PR) hard capsules, supplied in strengths of 8 mg, 16 mg, and 24 mg. Placebo capsules were visually identical to the active capsules in appearance, shape, and taste to maintain the blind.

-   **Test Drug:** Galantamine PR capsules (8, 16, and 24 mg)
-   **Batch Numbers:** GAL-PR-001A, GAL-PR-002B, GAL-PR-003C
-   **Placebo:** Matching placebo capsules
-   **Source:** All study medication was supplied by the sponsor.

Study medication was stored in a secure, limited-access area at the investigational site at a controlled room temperature between 15°C and 25°C (59°F to 77°F). No study materials were used past their expiry date.

# 9.4.3 Method of Assigning Patients to Treatment Groups

Patients were assigned to treatment groups according to a computer-generated randomisation schedule. An Interactive Web Response System (IWRS) was used to manage patient randomisation and drug dispensing. Authorised site personnel accessed the IWRS to randomise a patient, and the system assigned a randomisation number and a medication kit number for dispensing.

The randomisation was stratified by ApoE4 status (carrier vs. non-carrier) and by study centre to ensure a balanced distribution of these factors between the two treatment groups.

# 9.4.4 Selection of Doses in the Study

The dosing regimen was selected based on extensive prior clinical experience with the immediate-release (IR) formulation of galantamine. The 16 to 24 mg/day dose range was established as effective and generally well-tolerated for patients with mild to moderate AD. The prolonged-release formulation was designed to provide a smoother pharmacokinetic profile, potentially improving tolerability compared to the IR formulation. The slow dose-titration schedule, starting at 8 mg/day and increasing every 4 weeks, was implemented to minimise cholinergic side effects (e.g., nausea, vomiting) and improve overall tolerability.

# 9.4.5 Selection and Timing of Dose for Each Patient

All patients started on 8 mg/day. The dose was mandatorily increased to 16 mg/day at Week 4, provided there were no significant tolerability issues. At the Week 8 visit, the investigator made a clinical judgment as to whether to increase the dose to 24 mg/day or maintain the patient on 16 mg/day for the remainder of the study. This decision was based on the investigator's assessment of the patient's individual response and tolerability. Patients were instructed to take their study medication once daily in the morning, with food, to improve gastrointestinal tolerability.

# 9.4.6 Blinding

The study was conducted in a double-blind manner. Patients, investigators, site staff, and all sponsor representatives involved in the conduct or analysis of the study were blinded to the treatment allocation.

-   **Masking of Treatments:** Galantamine PR capsules and placebo capsules were identical in size, shape, color, and packaging. Bottles were labeled with the protocol number and a unique medication kit number.
-   **Unblinding:** The randomisation code for a specific patient was to be broken only in a medical emergency where knowledge of the treatment was essential for the patient's management. The procedure for emergency unblinding involved contacting the sponsor's designated 24-hour medical contact. All instances of unblinding were to be documented. There were no planned circumstances for unblinding all patients prior to study completion.
-   **Maintenance of Blinding:** No study personnel involved in the efficacy or safety assessments had access to the randomisation codes until the database was locked. Laboratory personnel performing standard safety tests were not aware of treatment assignments.

# 9.4.7 Prior and Concomitant Therapy

The use of drugs known to have anticholinergic properties or to be strong inhibitors/inducers of CYP2D6 or CYP3A4 was prohibited. The use of memantine and other acetylcholinesterase inhibitors was also prohibited.

Permitted concomitant medications for co-existing medical conditions were allowed at the discretion of the investigator, provided they were not expected to interfere with the study's primary endpoints. All prior and concomitant medications were recorded in the Case Report Form (CRF) from 14 days before the start of the double-blind treatment period until the final study visit. The potential for these medications to affect the study outcome was considered minimal due to the randomisation process, which was expected to balance their use between the treatment groups.

# 9.4.8 Treatment Compliance

Treatment compliance was assessed at each study visit by counting the number of returned capsules from the previously dispensed bottle. Patients were also provided with a diary to record the time of dose administration each day. Compliance was calculated as the percentage of expected doses taken. A patient was considered compliant if they took between 80% and 120% of the prescribed doses.

# 9.5.2 Appropriateness of Measurements

All efficacy and safety instruments used in this study are well-validated, widely used, and recognised as reliable and relevant for assessing the core symptoms of Alzheimer's Disease. The ADAS-cog is the standard primary outcome measure for cognition in AD trials. The CIBIC-plus, ADCS-ADL, and NPI are standard tools for assessing global change, activities of daily living, and neuropsychiatric symptoms, respectively. No surrogate endpoints were used.

# 9.5.3 Primary Efficacy Variable(s)

The primary efficacy variable was the change from baseline in the 11-item ADAS-cog score at Week 26. The ADAS-cog is a validated instrument for assessing the severity of cognitive dysfunction in AD. It consists of 11 tasks that assess memory, language, and praxis, with a total score ranging from 0 to 70; higher scores indicate greater impairment. This endpoint was chosen as it is the most widely accepted primary cognitive outcome measure in AD clinical trials and was recommended by regulatory authorities. The primary endpoint was pre-specified in the protocol.

# 9.5.4 Drug Concentration Measurements

No pharmacokinetic blood samples were collected for the measurement of galantamine concentrations in this study, as the pharmacokinetic profile of galantamine PR has been well-characterised in previous studies.

# 9.6 Data Quality Assurance

Several measures were implemented to ensure the quality and integrity of the study data. All investigators and study coordinators received training on the protocol, electronic Case Report Form (eCRF) completion, and study procedures during investigator meetings and site initiation visits.

Clinical research associates (CRAs) from the sponsor monitored the conduct of the study at each site. Monitoring visits were conducted to verify adherence to the protocol, Good Clinical Practice (GCP), and applicable regulatory requirements. The CRAs performed source data verification, which involved comparing the data in the eCRFs with the source documents at the site.

Data management was performed according to the sponsor's standard operating procedures. The electronic data capture (EDC) system had built-in checks to minimise data entry errors. A central laboratory was used for all clinical laboratory tests to ensure consistency and standardisation of results across sites. An independent internal audit was conducted by the sponsor's quality assurance department.

# 9.7 Statistical Methods Planned in the Protocol and Determination of Sample Size

# 9.7.1 Statistical and Analytical Plans

All statistical analyses were pre-specified in the Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP), which was finalised before the database lock and unblinding of the study.

The primary efficacy analysis was to be performed on the Intent-to-Treat (ITT) population, defined as all randomised patients who received at least one dose of study medication and had a baseline and at least one post-baseline ADAS-cog assessment. The primary endpoint, the change from baseline in ADAS-cog/11 score at Week 26, was to be analysed using an Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA) model with treatment and ApoE4 stratum as factors and baseline ADAS-cog/11 score as a covariate. Missing data for the primary analysis were to be imputed using the Last Observation Carried Forward (LOCF) method.

Secondary efficacy variables were to be analysed in a similar manner. Safety analyses were to be performed on the Safety population, defined as all patients who received at least one dose of study medication. AEs were to be coded using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA) and summarised by treatment group.

No interim analyses for efficacy or futility were planned.

# 9.7.2 Determination of Sample Size

The sample size was calculated to provide sufficient power for the primary efficacy endpoint. Assuming a treatment difference of 3.0 points on the ADAS-cog/11 scale between galantamine PR and placebo, with a common standard deviation of 6.5 points, a sample size of 175 patients per treatment group was required to provide 90% power to detect this difference using a two-sided t-test at an alpha level of 0.05. To account for a potential dropout rate of approximately 15%, the study planned to randomise a total of 400 patients (200 per group).

# 9.8 Changes in the Conduct of the Study or Planned Analyses

There was one major amendment to the protocol (Amendment 1, dated 15 Jan 2022) after the study had commenced but before any patients had completed the study. The amendment clarified the procedures for handling concomitant medications, specifically providing a more detailed list of prohibited medications. This change was implemented to enhance patient safety and reduce potential drug-drug interactions. The decision was made by the sponsor's medical team and was not based on any review of unblinded study data. This amendment was not expected to have any significant impact on the interpretation of the study results. No other significant changes were made to the study design, conduct, or planned statistical analyses after the initiation of the study.
