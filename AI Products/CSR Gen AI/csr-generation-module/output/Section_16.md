# 16.13 Listing of Discontinued Patients

This appendix provides a listing of all patients who discontinued from the study, along with the reasons for their discontinuation.

# 16.13.1 Reasons for Participant Discontinuation/Withdrawal

Participants may discontinue study intervention or withdraw from the study at any time. Reasons for definitive discontinuation of study intervention include adverse events, participant request, investigator request, pregnancy, or protocol deviation (e.g., no longer meeting inclusion criteria or meeting exclusion criteria, or a COVID-19 diagnosis). It is preferred that the second dose be administered unless considered unsafe or if the participant does not wish to receive it. Discontinuation of study intervention does not automatically mean withdrawal from the study; participants are generally followed for safety, immunogenicity, and efficacy.

Reasons for participant withdrawal from the study include:
-   Refused further follow-up
-   Lost to follow-up
-   Death
-   Study terminated by sponsor
-   Adverse Events (AEs)
-   Participant request
-   Investigator request
-   Protocol deviation

In instances of a participant failing to attend a required study visit, efforts are made to regain contact and reschedule. If a participant remains unreachable after documented attempts, they are considered to have withdrawn from the study.

\newpage

# 16.13.2 Summary of Adverse Events Leading to Study Withdrawal

Among all 43,448 enrolled participants included in the safety database up to the data cutoff date, a small number of participants were withdrawn from the study due to AEs. Specifically, 0.2% of participants in the BNT162b2 group and 0.1% in the placebo group were withdrawn due to AEs. These numbers are consistent with the population with a median of 2 months of follow-up after Dose 2 (0.2% in the BNT162b2 group and 0.1% in the placebo group).

The most frequently reported AEs leading to study withdrawal were in the System Organ Classes (SOCs) of gastrointestinal disorders; general disorders and administration site conditions; injury, poisoning, and procedural complications; musculoskeletal and connective tissue disorders; and nervous system disorders. No participants in the 16 to 17 years of age group experienced an AE leading to withdrawal. No clinically meaningful differences in AEs leading to withdrawal were observed by age or other subgroups.
