# 11. Efficacy Evaluation

# 11.1 Efficacy Analysis Populations

The efficacy analysis in this study primarily utilized two distinct populations: the Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP) and the All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data (ARSOD) population. The definitions of these populations were established prior to database lock and unblinding, as per the study protocol.

The **Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP)** includes all randomized subjects who received both doses of the investigational product or placebo, had no major protocol deviations affecting efficacy assessment, and were seronegative for SARS-CoV-2 at baseline. This population is considered the primary analysis population for demonstrating vaccine efficacy.

The **All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data (ARSOD) population** includes all randomized subjects who received at least one dose of the investigational product or placebo and had at least one on-treatment efficacy observation. This population serves as a supportive analysis to ensure consistency of findings and to mitigate potential biases that might arise from excluding patients due to compliance or other reasons.

# 11.1.1 Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria for Efficacy Data Sets

The inclusion and exclusion criteria for defining the efficacy data sets were explicitly outlined in the study protocol. These criteria were developed and finalized before any unblinding of the treatment assignments occurred, ensuring that the selection of analysis populations was unbiased. No post-unblinding modifications were made to the definitions of the efficacy populations.

The primary efficacy endpoint was the first occurrence of COVID-19 from 7 days after Dose 2 in subjects without evidence of prior infection. Secondary endpoints included the first occurrence of COVID-19 after Dose 1.

# 11.5 Statistical Methods and Analytical Issues

# 11.5.1 Statistical Methods

The statistical analysis methods employed in this study were pre-specified in the study protocol and statistical analysis plan (SAP). The primary efficacy analysis used a Cox proportional hazards model to estimate vaccine efficacy and its associated 95% confidence interval. This model accounted for stratification factors defined in the randomization scheme. Hypothesis testing was performed using a one-sided alpha level of 0.025 for the primary endpoint.

For continuous variables, analyses typically involved comparing means using t-tests or ANOVA, while categorical responses were analyzed using chi-square tests or Fisher's exact tests. Appropriate adjustments for multiple comparisons were applied where necessary to control the overall Type I error rate. Detailed documentation of statistical methods is available in Appendix 16.1.9.

# 11.5.2 Handling of Dropouts and Missing Data

All randomized patients with any on-treatment data were included in the 'All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data' (ARSOD) analysis, which served as a supportive analysis to the primary Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP) analysis. This approach ensured that the effects of dropouts were considered, and conclusions were not solely based on patients who completed the study.

The reasons for dropouts, time to dropout, and the proportion of dropouts across treatment groups were carefully monitored and are detailed in Section 10. The primary efficacy analysis employed a time-to-event approach, where subjects were censored at the date of dropout if no event occurred, thereby accounting for varying follow-up times. Missing data for efficacy endpoints were handled according to the principle of "missing at random" where appropriate, and sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of results to different missing data assumptions. Specific procedures for imputation or derivation of missing data, along with underlying assumptions, are fully described in the statistical analysis plan (Appendix 16.1.9).

# 11.5.3 Multicentre Study Considerations

This was a multicentre study conducted in the USA. Comparability between centres was assessed for critical demographic and baseline characteristics, as well as efficacy outcomes. Analyses were performed to allow for centre differences with respect to response, and individual centre results are presented where centres had sufficient patient numbers to provide valuable insights. No qualitative or quantitative treatment-by-centre interaction was noted. Demographic, baseline, and efficacy data by centre are provided in Appendix 16.2.4 and Appendix 16.2.6.

# 11.5.4 Interim Analyses and Changes in Analysis

All interim analyses, both formal and informal, pre-planned or ad hoc, conducted by any study participant, sponsor staff member, or data monitoring group, are fully described in Appendix 16.1.9, including minutes of meetings and data reports reviewed. The need for statistical adjustment due to such analyses was addressed, and details of any operating instructions or procedures used for these analyses are documented. Data monitoring without code-breaking was also performed to ensure patient safety and study integrity, without increasing the Type I error rate. No changes in the analysis were made after blind-breaking that were not pre-specified or fully justified.

# 11.7 Efficacy Results

# 11.7.1 Time Course of Response

The time course of response was evaluated by analyzing vaccine efficacy at different time points following vaccination. As observed in Table 4, efficacy for COVID-19 occurrence from 7 days after Dose 2 remained consistently high across all evaluated time intervals (i.e., <2 Months, <4 Months, and ≥4 Months after Dose 2). No COVID-19 cases were observed in the vaccine group at any of these intervals, suggesting rapid and sustained protection.

# 11.7.2 Dose Response

This study was not primarily designed as a dose-response study. However, the available data were examined for any apparent relationship between dose (30 µg BNT162b2) and response. Given that only one active dose level was administered, a formal dose-response analysis was not feasible.

# 11.7.3 Relationship with Concomitant Therapy and Illness

No apparent relationship was observed between vaccine response and concomitant therapy maintained during the study or between vaccine response and baseline comorbidities or past illnesses. The consistent efficacy across subgroups with and without comorbidities, as presented in Table 5, supports this observation.

# 11.8 Conclusions Concerning Efficacy

The study demonstrated that BNT162b2 (30 µg) vaccine is highly efficacious in preventing COVID-19 in subjects aged 12 through 15 years. The primary efficacy analysis, conducted on the Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP), showed a vaccine efficacy of 100.0% (95% CI: 86.8%, 100.0%) for the first occurrence of COVID-19 from 7 days after Dose 2. This finding was consistently supported by analyses in the All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data (ARSOD) population and across various demographic and baseline subgroups.

The vaccine also demonstrated 100.0% efficacy for the first COVID-19 occurrence after Dose 1, suggesting early protection. The efficacy was sustained over the observed follow-up period, with no evidence of waning protection up to at least 4 months post-Dose 2. The critical statistical approaches, including time-to-event analysis and appropriate handling of missing data and dropouts, reinforced the robustness of these findings. No significant treatment-by-centre interactions were identified in this multicentre study. The overall benefit-risk assessment for the vaccine remains highly favorable given the demonstrated high efficacy and acceptable safety profile (as detailed in Section 12).

# 11.5 Statistical Methods and Analytical Issues

# 11.5.1 Statistical Methods

The statistical analysis methods employed in this study were pre-specified in the study protocol and statistical analysis plan (SAP). The primary efficacy analysis used a Cox proportional hazards model to estimate vaccine efficacy and its associated 95% confidence interval. This model accounted for stratification factors defined in the randomization scheme. Hypothesis testing was performed using a one-sided alpha level of 0.025 for the primary endpoint.

For continuous variables, analyses typically involved comparing means using t-tests or ANOVA, while categorical responses were analyzed using chi-square tests or Fisher's exact tests. Appropriate adjustments for multiple comparisons were applied where necessary to control the overall Type I error rate. Detailed documentation of statistical methods is available in Appendix 16.1.9.

# 11.5.2 Handling of Dropouts and Missing Data

All randomized patients with any on-treatment data were included in the 'All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data' (ARSOD) analysis, which served as a supportive analysis to the primary Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP) analysis. This approach ensured that the effects of dropouts were considered, and conclusions were not solely based on patients who completed the study.

The reasons for dropouts, time to dropout, and the proportion of dropouts across treatment groups were carefully monitored and are detailed in Section 10. The primary efficacy analysis employed a time-to-event approach, where subjects were censored at the date of dropout if no event occurred, thereby accounting for varying follow-up times. Missing data for efficacy endpoints were handled according to the principle of "missing at random" where appropriate, and sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of results to different missing data assumptions. Specific procedures for imputation or derivation of missing data, along with underlying assumptions, are fully described in the statistical analysis plan (Appendix 16.1.9).

# 11.5.3 Multicentre Study Considerations

This was a multicentre study conducted in the USA. Comparability between centres was assessed for critical demographic and baseline characteristics, as well as efficacy outcomes. Analyses were performed to allow for centre differences with respect to response, and individual centre results are presented where centres had sufficient patient numbers to provide valuable insights. No qualitative or quantitative treatment-by-centre interaction was noted. Demographic, baseline, and efficacy data by centre are provided in Appendix 16.2.4 and Appendix 16.2.6.

# 11.5.4 Interim Analyses and Changes in Analysis

All interim analyses, both formal and informal, pre-planned or ad hoc, conducted by any study participant, sponsor staff member, or data monitoring group, are fully described in Appendix 16.1.9, including minutes of meetings and data reports reviewed. The need for statistical adjustment due to such analyses was addressed, and details of any operating instructions or procedures used for these analyses are documented. Data monitoring without code-breaking was also performed to ensure patient safety and study integrity, without increasing the Type I error rate. No changes in the analysis were made after blind-breaking that were not pre-specified or fully justified.

# 11.7 Efficacy Results

# 11.7.1 Time Course of Response

The time course of response was evaluated by analyzing vaccine efficacy at different time points following vaccination. As observed in Table 4, efficacy for COVID-19 occurrence from 7 days after Dose 2 remained consistently high across all evaluated time intervals (i.e., <2 Months, <4 Months, and ≥4 Months after Dose 2). No COVID-19 cases were observed in the vaccine group at any of these intervals, suggesting rapid and sustained protection.

# 11.7.2 Dose Response

This study was not primarily designed as a dose-response study. However, the available data were examined for any apparent relationship between dose (30 µg BNT162b2) and response. Given that only one active dose level was administered, a formal dose-response analysis was not feasible.

# 11.7.3 Relationship with Concomitant Therapy and Illness

No apparent relationship was observed between vaccine response and concomitant therapy maintained during the study or between vaccine response and baseline comorbidities or past illnesses. The consistent efficacy across subgroups with and without comorbidities, as presented in Table 5, supports this observation.

# 11.8 Conclusions Concerning Efficacy

The study demonstrated that BNT162b2 (30 µg) vaccine is highly efficacious in preventing COVID-19 in subjects aged 12 through 15 years. The primary efficacy analysis, conducted on the Efficacy Measurable Population (EMP), showed a vaccine efficacy of 100.0% (95% CI: 86.8%, 100.0%) for the first occurrence of COVID-19 from 7 days after Dose 2. This finding was consistently supported by analyses in the All Randomized Subjects with On-treatment Data (ARSOD) population and across various demographic and baseline subgroups.

The vaccine also demonstrated 100.0% efficacy for the first COVID-19 occurrence after Dose 1, suggesting early protection. The efficacy was sustained over the observed follow-up period, with no evidence of waning protection up to at least 4 months post-Dose 2. The critical statistical approaches, including time-to-event analysis and appropriate handling of missing data and dropouts, reinforced the robustness of these findings. No significant treatment-by-centre interactions were identified in this multicentre study. The overall benefit-risk assessment for the vaccine remains highly favorable given the demonstrated high efficacy and acceptable safety profile (as detailed in Section 12).
