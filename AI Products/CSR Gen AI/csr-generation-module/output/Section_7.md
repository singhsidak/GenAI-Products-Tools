# 7. Introduction

In December 2019, an outbreak of pneumonia of unknown cause in Wuhan, China, was identified as being caused by a novel coronavirus, later named Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). The resulting disease, Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19), spread rapidly across the globe, and on 11 March 2020, the World Health Organization (WHO) characterized the outbreak as a pandemic. At the time this study was initiated, there were no licensed vaccines or effective antiviral drugs available to prevent or treat COVID-19, representing a critical unmet medical need and a global public health crisis.

The investigational product, BNT162b2, is an RNA-based vaccine candidate developed to prevent COVID-19. This technology was selected for its potential to enable more rapid development and manufacturing compared to traditional vaccine approaches, a pivotal capability in a pandemic scenario. The vaccine consists of a nucleoside-modified messenger RNA (modRNA) that encodes the full-length, prefusion spike glycoprotein of the SARS-CoV-2 virus. When administered, the recipient's cells express the spike protein, which in turn is intended to elicit a protective immune response, including the generation of virus-neutralizing antibodies and a specific T-cell mediated immune response.

This study (C4591001) was a pivotal Phase 1/2/3 trial designed to provide a rapid assessment of the safety, tolerability, immunogenicity, and efficacy of the BNT162b2 vaccine. The primary aim was to evaluate the efficacy of a two-dose regimen of BNT162b2 in preventing COVID-19 in a broad population. The study initially enrolled healthy adults and was later expanded to include adolescents and individuals with stable chronic medical conditions to ensure the study population was as representative and diverse as possible. Participants were randomized to receive two intramuscular injections of either 30 µg of BNT162b2 or a saline placebo, administered 21 days apart. The total planned duration of follow-up for each participant was 24 months post-vaccination to continue gathering long-term safety and efficacy data.

The vaccine development program was conducted in close consultation with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and other global regulators. The study was designed and conducted in accordance with applicable guidance documents from the FDA, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and the WHO, as well as the principles of Good Clinical Practice (GCP) and the Declaration of Helsinki. The data from this study were intended to support an application for Emergency Use Authorization (EUA) and ultimately a Biologics License Application (BLA).
